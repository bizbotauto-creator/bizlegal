South Africa's Labour Law Wave: The 2025/2026 Amendment Bills and What They Mean for Your Business

April 2026
10 min read

South Africa's labour law landscape is undergoing its most significant transformation in several years. A convergence of factors — a Constitutional Court ruling on parental leave, comprehensive NEDLAC-negotiated amendment bills, a new Code of Good Practice on Dismissal, and increased minimum wage levels — means that virtually every employment relationship in the country is affected by changes that are either already in force or moving through Parliament.

On 27 February 2026, the Minister of Employment and Labour published the Labour Law Amendment Bill 2025 and the Labour Relations Amendment Bill 2025 for public comment. These bills propose 47 amendments to the Labour Relations Act 95 of 1995 (LRA), 13 amendments to the Basic Conditions of Employment Act 75 of 1997 (BCEA), two amendments to the National Minimum Wage Act, and three amendments to the Employment Equity Act. Together, they represent the most comprehensive round of labour law reform since the post-apartheid legislative framework was established in the mid-1990s.

This article explains the changes that are already in force, those that are imminent, and the practical steps every South African employer should take now to ensure their employment documentation and practices remain compliant.

The Most Significant Labour Law Reform in Years

The amendment bills are the product of extensive negotiations at the National Economic Development and Labour Council (NEDLAC), the tripartite body through which government, organised labour, and business engage on proposed labour legislation. NEDLAC's report, submitted to the Minister of Employment and Labour, captures the proposals tabled and the outcomes of those discussions.

It is important to note that while many of these proposals are far-reaching, the amendment bills are still subject to the legislative process. Public comment closes 30 days after publication, after which the State Law Advisor reviews the bills before they proceed through Parliament. Not all of the proposals had unanimous support from NEDLAC's social partners, which means further amendments may occur before the bills are enacted in their final form.

"These bills will materially reshape employer obligations, workplace flexibilities, and the management of employment disputes. The window for informal resolution is narrowing, and the cost of non-compliance is rising."

However, some changes do not require new legislation. The new Code of Good Practice on Dismissal (effective 4 September 2025), the National Minimum Wage increase (effective 1 March 2025), the parental leave changes (effective by Constitutional Court order from 3 October 2025), and the updated COIDA amendments (effective early 2026) are already in force and must be complied with immediately.

National Minimum Wage and Earnings Threshold Updates

Effective 1 March 2025, the National Minimum Wage increased from R27.58 to R28.79 per hour. This increase applies to the majority of employees, including farm workers and domestic workers. Learners enrolled under section 17 of the Skills Development Act are entitled to updated allowances as specified in the government schedule. Workers in expanded public works programmes earn a minimum of R15.83 per hour.

Employers who have not reviewed their payroll structures since early 2025 may already be in breach of the NMW. Non-compliance with the National Minimum Wage Act is an offence that carries administrative penalties, and enforcement is an explicit priority for the expanded inspectorate deployed following the 2026 State of the Nation Address.

The Earnings Threshold

The Minister of Employment and Labour updated the earnings threshold under the BCEA in April 2025. The earnings threshold determines which employees qualify for certain BCEA protections — including overtime pay, Sunday premium rates, and limits on working hours — that do not apply to higher-earning employees. Employers who have not reviewed which of their staff fall above or below the current threshold may be incorrectly applying BCEA leave and overtime provisions.

Action Required

Review Your Payroll and Employment Contracts

If your employment contracts specify a fixed hourly rate, or if any employees are paid at or near the minimum wage, immediate review is required. Contracts that reference the old NMW rate by number should be updated. Payroll systems must reflect the current rate for all pay runs from March 2025 onward.

The New Code of Good Practice on Dismissal

On 4 September 2025, a new Code of Good Practice on Dismissal officially replaced the previous code. This change is not proposed legislation — it is already in force and applies to every dismissal proceeding in South Africa.

The new Code introduces several changes that employers must understand and incorporate into their disciplinary procedures.

Incompatibility as a Ground for Dismissal

The new Code formally recognises incompatibility — the breakdown of the working relationship due to irreconcilable personality or cultural conflicts — as a fair ground for termination. This is a significant development for employers who have struggled to manage employees whose conduct does not rise to the level of misconduct but whose presence is genuinely disruptive to the workplace. Proper documentation and a fair process remain essential; incompatibility does not create a shortcut to dismissal.

Flexibility for Small Businesses

The new Code explicitly acknowledges that small businesses may not have formal HR teams or structured disciplinary procedures. It recognises that the procedural requirements applicable to large organisations may be disproportionately burdensome for an employer with fewer than ten employees, and allows for more flexible application of fair procedure in such circumstances. This does not remove the obligation to act fairly — it acknowledges that fairness may look different in a small business context.

Probation

The Code introduces a three-month probation period with clearer rules governing how performance during probation is assessed and managed. Employees on probation who are not meeting performance standards must be given notice of the shortfall and a reasonable opportunity to improve before termination is considered. The informality of this process is intended to make probationary management more straightforward for employers.

Opportunity to Respond

The Code clarifies that fair dismissal procedure requires giving the employee an adequate and reasonable opportunity to respond to the reasons for the proposed dismissal. This aligns with existing CCMA and Labour Court jurisprudence but codifies the expectation more explicitly, moving away from the highly formal, adversarial pre-dismissal hearing that many employers have historically used.

The New Parental Leave Framework

On 3 October 2025, the Constitutional Court handed down its judgment in Van Wyk and Others v Minister of Employment and Labour [2025] ZACC 20. The Court found that the existing BCEA provisions on parental leave were constitutionally defective in their discrimination between biological, adoptive, and commissioning parents, and between mothers and fathers.

As interim relief with immediate effect, the Constitutional Court ordered that all parents — biological, adoptive, and commissioning — are entitled to four months and ten days of parental leave, to be shared as they choose and taken either consecutively or concurrently. This interim order applies immediately and remains in force until Parliament enacts legislation remedying the constitutional defects. Parliament has 36 months from the date of judgment to do so.

Immediate Effect

Four Months and Ten Days — Shared by All Parents

With effect from 3 October 2025, all parents (biological, adoptive, and commissioning) are entitled to a total of four months and ten days of parental leave, to be shared as they choose. This applies to both parents and may be taken consecutively or concurrently. Employment contracts and HR policies that do not reflect this entitlement are immediately non-compliant.

Additionally, the proposed Labour Law Amendment Bill 2025 includes a redesigned, more inclusive parental leave regime that will eventually replace the current fragmented maternity, parental, adoption, and commissioning parental leave system. The new regime aims to be gender-neutral, reflecting the Court's direction.

What This Means for HR Policies

Every employer must review their leave policies and employment contracts immediately. Policies that reference the old parental leave framework — ten consecutive days for fathers, four months for mothers — are no longer accurate. Payroll systems that process parental leave claims must be configured to accommodate the new shared entitlement. The UIF structure governing parental leave claims will also need to be updated, and employers should monitor communications from the Department of Employment and Labour on how parental leave claims under the new framework are to be processed through the Fund.

Proposed Changes to Severance Pay

Among the most significant proposed amendments to the BCEA is a proposed increase in statutory severance pay from one week to two weeks of remuneration per completed year of service following retrenchment. This change would substantially increase the cost of retrenchment for employers, particularly those facing large-scale workforce reductions.

It is critical to understand that this change is proposed, not yet enacted. The enhanced entitlement would apply only to years of service completed after the amendments take effect — existing years of service would continue to attract the one-week rate. Employers currently engaged in retrenchment processes should seek legal advice on the implications of the proposed change and the timing of their processes.

Other Proposed BCEA Amendments

The Amendment Bill also proposes stricter enforcement mechanisms for benefit fund payment obligations, requiring employers to transfer benefit fund contributions on time and imposing financial penalties for late payments. This affects employers who contribute to pension funds, provident funds, and medical aid schemes on behalf of employees.

Gig and On-Call Workers: New Protections

One of the most consequential proposed changes in the Amendment Bills concerns workers in non-standard employment relationships — including gig workers (ride-hailing drivers, food delivery workers, freelancers on digital platforms) and on-call or zero-hours workers.

The Expanded Definition of "Employee"

The proposed amendments introduce a new schedule to the LRA extending rights to freedom of association, organisational rights, and bargaining rights to a broader category of workers. The proposed definition captures individuals who work personally for a person who is not a client or customer of any profession, business, or undertaking carried on by that individual. A presumption of employment applies unless the engaging party can demonstrate that certain factors are satisfied.

A corresponding amendment to the BCEA would allow the Minister to set basic conditions of employment — working hours, leave entitlements, minimum wages — for this expanded category of workers through sectoral determinations. For businesses that engage platform workers, delivery drivers, or freelancers, this proposed change has significant implications for how those relationships are structured and documented.

On-Call and Zero-Hours Workers

The Amendment Bill proposes new BCEA provisions governing employees who are required to hold themselves available for work but are not guaranteed work by their employers. Employers would be required to confirm in writing the notice period for calling an employee in to work, the notice period for any cancellation of work, and the employee's expected availability. Both the notice to report for work and the notice of cancellation must be reasonable. These protections are aimed at increasing income predictability and security for workers in precarious employment situations.

What Your Business Must Do Now

The scale and complexity of these changes make clear that a reactive approach — waiting until a dispute arises, a labour inspector knocks, or a CCMA referral is received — is insufficient. The following actions should be prioritised immediately.

  • Review and update all employment contracts. Contracts should reflect the current NMW, updated BCEA provisions (particularly hours, overtime, and leave entitlements), and the new parental leave framework. Contracts that reference the old parental leave entitlements, out-of-date notice periods, or hourly rates below the current NMW are non-compliant.
  • Update your HR and disciplinary policies to incorporate the new Code of Good Practice on Dismissal. Disciplinary procedures, performance management frameworks, and probation policy documents should all be revised to reflect the new Code's requirements.
  • Assess your parental leave policy for immediate compliance with the Constitutional Court's interim order. Communicate the updated entitlements to all employees. Configure your payroll and leave management systems accordingly.
  • Audit your benefit fund payment process. Ensure that contributions to pension funds, provident funds, and medical aid schemes are being transferred on time, in light of the proposed stricter enforcement provisions.
  • Review your engagement with platform workers and on-call staff. If your business engages workers who may fall within the proposed expanded definition of "employee", obtain legal advice on how those relationships should be structured under the proposed new framework.
  • Attend to COIDA registration and compliance. The 2026 COIDA amendments introduced updated compliance duties, administrative penalties, and expanded enforcement powers. Confirm that your registration is accurate, your injury reporting processes are current, and your records are maintained as required.

For most SMEs, many of these changes will require updating documentation that was prepared years ago and has not been reviewed since. A single consultation with a qualified labour practitioner — covering contracts, policies, and payroll structures against the current legislative requirements — is likely to be far less expensive than defending an unfair dismissal or NMW non-compliance claim before the CCMA or Labour Court.
